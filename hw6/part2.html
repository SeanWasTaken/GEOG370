Ratio

The ratio presented on these maps is the Estimated K-12 Enrollment in Private Schools / Estimated K-12 Enrollment. I created this ratio because I felt it would be interesting to see in which counties were private schools most popular. From this ratio I found that in the county I was born in more than a third of the students go to private schools.

Classification 1: Quantiles

This ratio is categorized using quantiles, where an equal amount of items are sorted into every class. This is good for emphasizing the position of data in a set, however it can also make small differences appear too great. For example, from the other maps of this ratio we know that counties in north east have a similar ratio, but this categorization makes that appearence appear greater because one of the counties is in the highest grouping.

Classification 2: Equal Interval

This ratio is categorized using equal intervals, where the attributes are divided into equal sized ranges. The advantage of this method is the creation of an easy to interpret legend, however it causes most data values to fall into one or two classes. This disadvantage of equal intervals can be seen on this map, as most counties are a light shade of green, with one county being very dark green.

Classification 3: Natural Breaks

This ratio is categorized using natural breaks, meaning the items are sorted into groups by the data is naturally arranged into. This method works well with data that is unevenly distributed without being skewed in a direction. The disadvantages of this method are the ranges greatly vary in size and it can be difficult to compare maps created with different natural breaks.
